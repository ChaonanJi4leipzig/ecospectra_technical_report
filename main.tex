\title{Trait-centered Sentinel-2 time-series learning from sparse hyperspectral labels: a systematic ablation of design choices and generalization}

\author{Chaonan Ji\corref{cor1}}
\cortext[cor1]{Corresponding author}
\ead{chaonan.ji@uvsq.fr}
\address{EcoSpectra traits-model project}

\begin{abstract}
Forest functional traits link canopy structure and leaf biochemistry to ecosystem functioning, carbon exchange, and biodiversity, but spatially explicit trait observations remain sparse at landscape to continental scales. Sentinel-2 provides dense optical time series that could extend sparse hyperspectral trait labels, yet the design choices required for reliable trait-centered learning are rarely isolated. We evaluate a framework that anchors supervised samples on finite hyperspectral trait labels and links them to Sentinel-2 temporal windows under shared preprocessing, model-selection, and transfer-evaluation protocols. The main EnMAP analysis covers 47 European forest sites and 230,397 trait-labelled events from 2022--2025. In a five-trait grouped benchmark, model skill was trait dependent: LAI reached the highest grouped performance ($R^2=0.604$, nRMSE $=9.0\%$ for MLP), whereas chlorophyll and lignin were weaker. In a separate 37-site quality-filtered three-trait sample-size benchmark, mean nRMSE decreased from 9.49\% at a maximum of 2,000 events per site-year to 7.96\% at 20,000 events per site-year for the complete XGBoost trajectory; Transformer improved from 9.44\% at 2,000 to 8.41\% at 5,000 events per site-year, but higher-cap neural rows differed in trait coverage and were not interpreted as a monotonic scaling curve. A sample-matched temporal-window experiment showed that 28 days gave the best mean performance among the evaluated windows ($R^2=0.497$, nRMSE $=10.39\%$). ERA5-Land climate context improved random forest and MLP performance ($\Delta R^2=0.027$ and 0.034, respectively) but had little average effect for the Transformer ($\Delta R^2=0.004$). Cross-year evaluation reduced mean $R^2$ to approximately 0.37 across model families, and cross-site evaluation to approximately 0.25; chlorophyll collapsed to near zero (mean $R^2=0.004$) under cross-site transfer. These results show that Sentinel-2 trait upscaling from sparse hyperspectral labels depends as much on representational and validation choices as on model architecture, and reframe transfer evaluation as a primary, not diagnostic, component of method assessment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Plant functional traits quantify the strategies by which vegetation captures resources, allocates biomass, and responds to environmental variation, and they provide a mechanistic link between species composition and ecosystem functioning \citep{wright2004leaf, diaz2016global}. Among these traits, leaf area index (LAI) governs canopy light interception and primary productivity; leaf mass per area (LMA), nitrogen, and lignin trace the leaf economics spectrum from acquisitive to conservative strategies \citep{wright2004leaf}; and chlorophyll content reflects short-term photosynthetic capacity and stress \citep{croft2017chlorophyll}. Together these five traits form a minimum set linking canopy structure, biochemistry, and physiological state, and are routinely targeted by ecosystem and remote-sensing studies \citep{jetz2016monitoring}.

Yet trait observations remain orders of magnitude sparser than the spatial and temporal heterogeneity they aim to describe. Global trait databases such as TRY contain millions of records and provide the main empirical foundation for trait-based ecology, but continuous traits remain incomplete and unevenly represented across species, environments, and space \citep{kattge2020try}. In situ campaigns also rarely revisit the same canopy through a full phenological cycle. This sparsity motivates remote-sensing-based upscaling, where dense satellite observations extend a limited set of ground, airborne, or spaceborne trait estimates.

Two complementary classes of optical satellites are now operational. Imaging spectroscopy missions such as EnMAP and PRISMA sample hundreds of narrow spectral bands and can retrieve canopy traits at scene scale through radiative-transfer inversion or empirical regression \citep{guanter2015enmap, cogliati2021prisma}. Their revisit cycles are sparse and their swaths are narrow. Sentinel-2, in contrast, provides 10--20 m multispectral observations every 2--5 days with near-global coverage \citep{drusch2012sentinel}. Its 13 broad bands constrain trait retrieval less directly than imaging spectroscopy, but its temporal density and continuity make it a practical backbone for landscape monitoring.

A natural research question follows: can sparse hyperspectral trait retrievals serve as supervised labels for learning trait predictions from dense Sentinel-2 time series? Related studies have shown that Sentinel-2 can retrieve LAI, chlorophyll, and other vegetation traits using radiative-transfer models and hybrid machine-learning workflows \citep{brown2019sentinel2, estevez2020gpr, estevez2021traits}. Deep sequence models have also advanced Sentinel-2 and broader satellite image time-series analysis through recurrent encoders and temporal self-attention, especially for land-cover and crop-mapping tasks \citep{russwurm2018multi, garnot2020sits}. However, the forest-trait prediction setting studied here has been less systematically explored with these architectures, and the sparse-to-dense learning problem also depends on how labelled events are sampled, how temporal observations are represented, and how models transfer across years and sites.

Three features distinguish this setting from standard supervised regression. Labels are irregular in both space and time, with no guarantee of overlap with any individual Sentinel-2 acquisition. Sentinel-2 observations themselves are subject to cloud-driven missingness, so the predictor sequence at each label is incomplete by construction. Because the scientific goal is to deploy the trained model to unseen years and sites, in-domain test performance is insufficient evidence: temporal and spatial transfer must be evaluated explicitly.

Four methodological gaps follow from these features. First, the construction of the supervised sample itself is rarely scrutinized. Common practice equates optically valid pixels with labelled observations, but a cloud-free Sentinel-2 pixel does not necessarily correspond to a trait measurement; conflating the two inflates effective sample size and may bias model selection. Second, the temporal representation of Sentinel-2---window length, observation density, and missing-value encoding---is typically chosen by convention rather than by ablation. Yet for vegetation variables with strong phenological dynamics, representational choices may rival model architecture in their effect on prediction accuracy \citep{russwurm2018multi, tian2021phenology}. Third, auxiliary information streams such as climate reanalysis are increasingly available for land-surface applications \citep{munozsabater2021era5land} and are often assumed to add mechanistic context to optical predictors. Whether this gain is universal or family-specific, particularly when architectures such as Transformers may already encode seasonal structure, remains untested in side-by-side trait ablations. Fourth, evaluation regimes are inconsistently reported. Grouped train/test splits estimate in-domain accuracy but say little about deployment on unseen years or sites, where covariate, label, and concept shifts may all change. Without principled temporal and spatial transfer evaluation, reported accuracies can overstate operational reliability.

We address these gaps through a systematic ablation study of trait-centered Sentinel-2 learning. Our contributions are threefold. First, we introduce a trait-centered sample construction that anchors supervision on finite hyperspectral trait labels and propagates strict train-only preprocessing, making the supervised contract independent of optical data availability. Second, we benchmark three canonical model families---random forest, sequence MLP, and Transformer---across five forest traits under shared sample and evaluation contracts, and show that auxiliary information gains are model-family specific: ERA5-Land climate context substantially helps random forest and MLP ($\Delta R^2=0.027$ and 0.034) but yields little Transformer improvement ($\Delta R^2=0.004$), consistent with the Transformer encoding seasonal structure directly from the optical sequence. Third, we define a generalization protocol and apply grouped, temporal-transfer, and spatial-transfer evaluations to the present EnMAP benchmark, demonstrating substantial drops in mean $R^2$ under both transfer regimes relative to grouped testing. Label-source sensitivity (EnMAP versus PRISMA) is reserved for a companion analysis once PRISMA trait products are available. Mean $R^2$ drops to approximately 0.37 across the three model families under temporal transfer and approximately 0.25 under spatial transfer, with chlorophyll near zero under cross-site evaluation. These findings reframe Sentinel-2 trait upscaling as governed jointly by representation, auxiliary information, and validation regime, not by model architecture alone.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~2 describes data sources and sites. Section~3 specifies the trait-centered learning framework, model families, generalization protocol, and statistical comparison. Section~4 reports baseline performance and four ablation streams: sample design, temporal representation, auxiliary information, and out-of-domain transfer. Section~5 discusses implications for Sentinel-2 trait upscaling.

\section{Data}

\subsection{Study sites and forest mask}

The main analysis covers 47 European forest sites spanning latitudes from $36.93^\circ$N to $68.00^\circ$N and longitudes from $8.00^\circ$W to $26.64^\circ$E (Fig.~\ref{fig:site_map}). Sites were selected from 50 EnMAP-compatible candidate sites for joint availability of Sentinel-2 acquisitions, EnMAP hyperspectral trait-label support, ERA5-Land climate reanalysis, and Copernicus land-cover products over 2022--2025. Each site is represented on a 30 m, approximately 30 km $\times$ 30 km analysis grid, giving an order-of-magnitude footprint of $4.2 \times 10^4$ km$^2$ before forest masking. The sites span Atlantic, temperate, Alpine, Mediterranean, boreal, broadleaf, coniferous, and mixed-forest contexts, providing variability in canopy structure, species composition, and climate regime.

Forest pixels are defined using the Copernicus High Resolution Layer Tree Cover Density 2021 product with a threshold of $TCD \geq 30\%$ \citep{copernicusHrlForest}. This is stricter than the FAO minimum forest-definition canopy-cover threshold of 10\% \citep{fao2020terms}, and was chosen to emphasize closed and semi-closed forest canopies rather than sparse tree cover. Sensitivity to the TCD threshold is reserved for Supplement S1. Three sites were excluded before model construction because their land-cover support is incomplete for the analysis period, reducing the working set from 50 EnMAP-compatible sites to 47 retained sites; these excluded sites account for 14 of 952 indexed EnMAP scene-site intersections (1.5\%), used here as a scene-level proxy for candidate label support. As a secondary mask, the ESA WorldCover 10 m product is retained only for sensitivity comparisons reported in Supplement S2 and for the separate 37-site quality-filtered cohort; it does not define the primary forest mask.

\subsection{Trait labels and predictor streams}

Trait labels are sparse hyperspectral trait estimates imported from EnMAP Level-2A surface-reflectance trait-region products at 30 m spatial resolution \citep{guanter2015enmap}. These products were generated upstream of the Sentinel-2 modelling experiment by applying the transferable multi-trait hyperspectral retrieval workflow of \citet{cherif2023spectra} to EnMAP scenes on their acquisition dates. The retrieval model is a one-dimensional convolutional neural network adapted from EfficientNet-B0 for spectra, trained on 42 heterogeneous canopy-spectra data sets with sparse in situ trait labels. The source spectra were harmonized to 400--2500 nm, water-absorption regions were removed, spectra were smoothed with a Savitzky--Golay filter, and 20 traits were learned jointly with a weakly supervised loss that updates only finite trait targets in the baseline CNNmultiIncomplete configuration. For EnMAP inference, the package resamples EnMAP band reflectance to the model wavelength metadata and back-transforms the 20 predicted traits to physical units; the present Sentinel-2 study treats those retrievals as fixed labels and does not re-fit the hyperspectral retrieval model. All five focal traits used in this study are in their reported physical units after back-transformation---LAI in m$^2$ m$^{-2}$, LMA in g m$^{-2}$, N in mg cm$^{-2}$, Chl in $\mu$g cm$^{-2}$, and lignin in mg cm$^{-2}$---and no additional unit conversion is applied between the EnMAP retrieval output and the Sentinel-2 supervised target. The five focal traits are leaf area index (LAI, m$^2$ m$^{-2}$; 5--95\% range 1.74--4.20), leaf mass per area (LMA, g m$^{-2}$; 60.9--137.8), foliar nitrogen content (N, mg cm$^{-2}$; 0.120--0.221), chlorophyll content (Chl, $\mu$g cm$^{-2}$; 27.1--43.2), and lignin content (mg cm$^{-2}$; 0.781--2.880). These ranges are empirical 5--95\% quantiles from the 230,397 retained EnMAP-labelled events and are summarized in Supplement Fig. S3.

Sentinel-2 Level-2A surface reflectance observations \citep{drusch2012sentinel} provide the predictor time series. We use ten 10 m and 20 m bands sampled onto the trait-event grid: B02, B03, B04, B05, B06, B07, B08, B8A, B11, and B12. The predictor stack per Sentinel-2 acquisition comprises (i) those surface-reflectance bands; (ii) seven vegetation and moisture/burn indices (NDVI, EVI, NDWI, NBR, CIRE, NDRE, and MSI; formulas to be reported in Supplement Table S2); (iii) the temporal offset from the trait label date plus cyclic day-of-year features ($\sin$ and $\cos$ of DOY); and (iv) observation-validity mask/weight channels. Missing observations within the temporal window are zero-filled in reflectance and index channels and indicated by the mask, allowing models to distinguish absent observations from genuinely low reflectance.

Climate context is drawn from ERA5-Land daily nearest-point data \citep{munozsabater2021era5land}. For each trait-labelled event, we extract 2 m temperature maximum, 2 m temperature minimum, total precipitation, downward surface solar radiation, and volumetric soil water in layer 1 as an optional eight-slot, 14-day aggregate sequence ending at the label date. ERA5-Land covariates are evaluated as an auxiliary stream in the climate ablation rather than as default predictors.

\subsection{Analysis cohorts}

The manuscript separates two analysis cohorts to avoid conflating data contracts. The primary five-trait EnMAP cohort contains 47 sites and 230,397 trait-labelled events after the HRL forest mask and land-cover support filters, and is used to summarize baseline model-family behavior and the ERA5-Land paired ablation.

A secondary 37-site quality-filtered cohort is used for sample-size, temporal-window, and temporal/spatial transfer analyses. This cohort is built from the WorldCover-filtered high-cap sample banks on the 37 sites listed in the site-map overlay and contains 196,144 events at the 2,000 events per site-year cap and 1,933,048 events at the 20,000 cap. It focuses on LAI, LMA, and chlorophyll because these traits have the most complete support in the filtered high-cap design; nitrogen and lignin are retained in the primary five-trait cohort but are not interpreted in the current high-cap transfer figures. Results from the two cohorts are therefore interpreted as separate evidence streams rather than pooled into one aggregate score. \section{Methods}

\subsection{Overview of the trait-centered learning framework}

The framework treats each finite trait-label event as the supervised anchor and links it to Sentinel-2 observations in a bounded temporal window (Fig.~\ref{fig:framework}). This design separates four axes that can be varied independently in ablation: the supervision source, the predictor representation, the model family, and the evaluation regime. Each ablation isolates one axis by varying it while holding the other axes fixed at their default configurations (Table~\ref{tab:contract}), allowing the marginal effect of each design choice to be attributed cleanly.

\caption{Trait-centered learning framework. Sparse hyperspectral trait labels define supervised events; Sentinel-2 observations provide temporal optical predictors; model families are evaluated under grouped in-domain, temporal-transfer, and spatial-transfer regimes; and ablations test sample size, temporal representation, climate context, and transfer split design.}
\label{fig:framework}

\subsection{Trait-centered sample construction}

The training unit is a trait-labelled event indexed by site, year, pixel coordinates, label date, and label source, with a per-event mask indicating which of the five focal traits are finite. Events with no finite trait are discarded; events with at least one finite trait are retained and contribute only to the loss of their non-missing targets. For each event, Sentinel-2 acquisitions falling within the default $\pm 14$-day window of the label date are assembled into a temporal sequence of up to $T_{\max}=8$ slots, ordered by absolute temporal offset to the label date with the closest observation first. Any learned preprocessing, including imputers, feature scaling, and target transformations, is fit on the training partition only and then applied to validation and test partitions. Label provenance is stored per event to support supplementary label-source sensitivity analysis.

\subsection{Predictor representation}

The default representation is an eight-slot Sentinel-2 temporal sequence. Observations are ordered by absolute temporal proximity to the hyperspectral label date. When fewer than $T_{\max}$ valid acquisitions are available, the remaining slots are zero-filled in the reflectance and index channels and marked as invalid in the sequence mask. The observation-validity weight is also included as a feature channel. For tree and MLP models, temporal slots and their mask channels are flattened into a tabular vector; for the Transformer, the same mask suppresses attention to invalid tokens and the final temporal readout is a masked mean over valid slots.

Temporal-window ablations compare the default 28-day window against 0.5-, 7-, 14-, 28-, 60-, 90-, 180-, and 365-day alternatives. To avoid confounding window length with event availability, the sample-matched window experiment operates on the same cohort of trait-labelled events in all arms; only the Sentinel-2 acquisitions assigned to each event change.

\subsection{Model architectures}

We compare three canonical model families. Random forest \citep{breiman2001random} serves as the non-neural tree baseline. The eight-slot temporal sequence is flattened into a tabular feature vector by concatenating slots along the feature dimension; missing-slot mask channels are passed as additional features. The random forest uses 1,000 trees, with trait-specific maximum feature fraction and minimum leaf size selected by validation nRMSE.

The sequence MLP consumes the same flattened representation as random forest. The architecture used in the reported five-trait benchmark is a four-layer feedforward network with 64 hidden units per layer, ReLU activations, dropout $p=0.3$, weight decay $=10^{-3}$, and learning rate $=10^{-3}$. It produces one trait prediction per event in single-trait runs, while the shared multi-output runner uses the same selective masking of missing targets in the loss.

The Transformer applies self-attention over the eight temporal slots following \citet{vaswani2017attention}. Each slot token is the concatenation of reflectance bands, indices, time features, and observation-weight channel. The reported five-trait benchmark uses two encoder layers, four attention heads, model dimension $d_{\mathrm{model}}=256$, dropout $p=0.3$, weight decay $=10^{-4}$, and learning rate $=3 \times 10^{-4}$. The invalid-slot mask is applied to self-attention weights and to the final masked-mean temporal pooling.

Random forest, MLP, and Transformer constitute the canonical model set for the grouped five-trait benchmark and ERA5 paired ablation. For the sample-size and transfer ablations, XGBoost is reported in place of random forest because gradient boosting scales more efficiently to the high-cap (20,000 events per site-year) and split-heavy (8-cluster spatial transfer and 12-configuration temporal transfer) experimental design. These XGBoost rows should be interpreted as tree-family evidence, not as a replacement for the canonical random-forest baseline. \subsection{Hyperparameter selection}

Hyperparameters are selected by grid search using validation nRMSE under the same split and sample contract as the corresponding experiment. The tree-model grid includes the number of estimators, maximum depth, minimum leaf or child weight, feature fraction, subsampling fraction, and regularization strength; the neural grid includes depth, width or model dimension, attention-head count for the Transformer, dropout, learning rate, batch size, and weight decay. The full grid and per-trait selections are reported in Supplement Table S4. Model-family settings are then fixed before test evaluation, and the test partition is used only for final reporting. For neural models, early stopping is based on validation RMSE. For tree models, the selected estimator configuration is refit on the training partition and evaluated on the held-out test partition with the same target transformation and preprocessing contract. All reported split generation and model initialisation seeds are fixed at seed 42 unless otherwise stated.

\subsection{Auxiliary information protocols}

ERA5-Land climate context is constructed as a past-only auxiliary stream matched to each trait-labelled event. The ERA5 sequence contains eight 14-day aggregate slots ordered oldest to newest, with the newest aggregate ending at the label date; no post-label climate information enters the predictor stream. This constraint is required for operational use, where forecast-time deployment cannot access future climate. The climate ablation pairs each trait, model family, and split configuration between two arms: Sentinel-2-only and Sentinel-2-plus-ERA5. Both arms share identical events, splits, and hyperparameter search spaces; the only difference is the inclusion or exclusion of the ERA5 covariate block.

\subsection{Generalization protocol}

We distinguish three primary evaluation regimes in the main text. The grouped in-domain regime randomly partitions the full pool of trait-labelled events into 70/15/15 train/validation/test splits, with grouping by pixel identity to prevent the same pixel appearing across partitions. This regime estimates performance when train and test events are drawn from the same overall labelled population.

The temporal-transfer regime holds out one calendar year at a time as the test set, with a separate year used for validation and the remaining two years used for training. We evaluate the twelve train/validation/test year configurations available within the 2022--2025 period and report mean performance across configurations; per-configuration results are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:temporal_generalization}. The spatial-transfer regime holds out geographic site clusters that approximate broad biogeographic structure across Europe but do not explicitly encode climate variables, forest type, or species composition. For the current 37-site transfer experiment, clusters are generated by $k$-means on standardized site longitude and latitude, with random seed 42 and 50 initializations. Candidate values $k=8,\ldots,15$ were evaluated; the selected $k=8$ is the largest candidate with no singleton clusters, producing cluster sizes of 2--9 sites and silhouette score 0.465. Each fold uses one cluster as test and the next cluster id cyclically as validation, with all remaining clusters used for training. Cluster membership and the candidate-$k$ diagnostics are reported in Supplement Table S5. A label-source sensitivity analysis comparing EnMAP-only and pooled EnMAP+PRISMA supervision is reserved for Supplement S6 after PRISMA trait-region products are available.

\subsection{Statistical comparison protocol}

Ablation effects are reported as paired differences when treatment and baseline share the same trait, split, and model family. We use $\Delta R^2$ and $\Delta$NRMSE, with positive $\Delta R^2$ and negative $\Delta$NRMSE indicating improvement. For the ERA5-Land paired ablation, 95\% confidence intervals are estimated by bootstrap resampling across the five paired trait rows within each model family. These intervals describe trait-level variability in the current ablation and should not be interpreted as a residual bootstrap over pixels. Transfer heatmaps are reported as descriptive split-level summaries because they do not yet include a paired residual bootstrap. \section{Results}

\subsection{Baseline performance}

We first establish baseline performance across the five focal traits and three canonical model families under the default sample contract (Table~\ref{tab:contract}). This benchmark serves as the reference against which subsequent ablations are paired. Performance varies strongly by trait and by model family, indicating that a single aggregate accuracy score would obscure meaningful structure in the results. MLP and RF rank comparably for several traits, whereas the Transformer is slightly weaker in this in-domain benchmark. \subsection{Design choices: data volume and temporal window}

The sample-size ablation tests whether prediction is limited by labelled-data volume in the 37-site quality-filtered three-trait cohort. We vary the per-site-year sample cap from 2,000 to 20,000 events while holding all other design choices fixed. This ablation uses LAI, LMA, and chlorophyll because these traits have the most complete support in the filtered high-cap design. Mean test nRMSE decreases from 9.49\% at a cap of 2,000 to 7.96\% at a cap of 20,000 for the complete XGBoost trajectory. Transformer improves from 9.44\% at a cap of 2,000 to 8.41\% at a cap of 5,000. The apparent cap-10,000 Transformer value is not interpreted as part of the main three-trait curve because that row contains only two completed traits, while the cap-2,000 and cap-5,000 rows contain all three. The defensible conclusion is therefore that sample scaling improves the complete XGBoost trajectory and improves Transformer through 5,000 events per site-year, but the available neural rows do not establish monotonic high-cap scaling. The sample-matched temporal-window experiment controls for event availability by restricting all window lengths to the same sample cohort. Window lengths of 0.5, 7, 14, 28, 60, 90, 180, and 365 days were compared on 53,546 shared events using the Transformer and the three-trait filtered cohort. This non-monotonic pattern indicates that the apparent gains often attributed to longer temporal context can reflect event-count differences in unmatched comparisons; under sample-matched conditions, the trade-off between phenological signal and acquisition relevance favors an intermediate window.

\subsection{Auxiliary information streams}

ERA5-Land context improves the random forest and MLP more consistently than the Transformer. \section{Discussion}

The main result is that Sentinel-2 trait learning from sparse hyperspectral labels is governed by representation and validation choices as much as by model architecture. This extends earlier trait-mapping and biophysical-retrieval work, which emphasized the potential of optical spectroscopy and machine-learning retrieval but also highlighted scaling, uncertainty, and operational robustness as persistent challenges \citep{homolova2013review, verrelst2015review}. Anchoring supervised samples on finite trait labels rather than optically valid pixels is a deceptively simple choice with material consequences: the retained EnMAP contract contains 230,397 finite trait-labelled events, whereas the upstream optical candidate space is orders of magnitude larger. This distinction ensures that downstream ablations measure model behavior on genuine trait supervision rather than on optical coverage.

The sample-matched temporal-window ablation refines a common heuristic in time-series remote sensing \citep{russwurm2018multi, tian2021phenology}. Longer temporal windows are often assumed to improve accuracy by capturing seasonal context; our results show that under sample-matched conditions, the optimum lies at an intermediate window of 28 days in the current three-trait Transformer experiment, and that gains from longer windows in unmatched comparisons may largely reflect increased event availability rather than additional phenological information.

The ERA5-Land ablation shows that auxiliary information is model-family specific. The stronger gains for random forest and MLP suggest that explicit climate covariates can compensate for weaker temporal inductive bias. The weaker Transformer response is consistent with partial redundancy between climate covariates and phenological information already encoded in the Sentinel-2 sequence, but direct evidence for this mechanism remains to be developed through attention-pattern visualization or linear probing of climate variables from learned representations. This distinction matters for method design: adding climate context should be justified by paired ablations rather than adopted as a universal default.

The transfer experiments demonstrate that grouped in-domain accuracy is not sufficient evidence for robustness. Temporal transfer primarily tests year-to-year differences in phenology, climate, and label distribution, whereas spatial transfer also tests changes in forest composition, background conditions, and reflectance--trait relationships. The present transfer figures should therefore be read as robustness screening rather than a complete failure-mode attribution.

The chlorophyll result is the clearest operational warning in the current benchmark. Its grouped performance is already lower than LAI, and its cross-site $R^2$ collapses to 0.004 in the 37-site split. Several mechanisms could contribute: a narrower effective dynamic range in the EnMAP-derived labels, species-specific pigment--reflectance responses, phenological timing mismatch between pigment state and available Sentinel-2 acquisitions, and the retrieval label noise floor inherited from the upstream hyperspectral model. Until site-specific calibration or stronger cross-site validation is available, chlorophyll predictions from this framework should be treated as reliable only within represented site contexts and should not be deployed to unseen forest contexts without site-specific calibration or independent validation.

The numerical comparison with earlier Sentinel-2 trait retrieval studies is instructive. Our best grouped LAI $R^2$ of 0.604 is comparable to the lower-to-middle part of the forest LAI literature: \citet{meyer2019landsatSentinel} reported a best Sentinel-2 LAI model explaining 59.3\% of variance in a central European broadleaf forest, whereas \citet{brown2019sentinel2} reported $r^2=0.54$ for the SNAP L2B LAI algorithm and $r^2=0.79$ for an INFORM-based Sentinel-2 forest LAI retrieval. However, our cross-site LAI $R^2$ of 0.469 reveals a transfer gap rarely quantified in single-site or grouped-validation studies. For chlorophyll, our grouped $R^2$ of 0.38 is below the site-optimized canopy chlorophyll performance reported by \citet{brown2019sentinel2} ($r^2=0.52$ for SNAP L2B CCC and 0.69 for INFORM-based CCC) and \citet{zarco2019chlorophyll} ($r^2=0.71$ for Sentinel-2A INFORM retrieval in an open conifer canopy), but is closer to the heterogeneous spruce-stand Sentinel-2 leaf chlorophyll result of \citet{darvishzadeh2019chlorophyll} ($R^2=0.45$). We interpret this gap as a consequence of the broader site, species, and phenological variation in the European-scale label set, which limits the model's ability to exploit site-specific spectral signatures.

The conclusions should also be read with explicit robustness boundaries. The model-family-specific auxiliary-value finding would be revised if the Transformer were systematically undertrained relative to RF and MLP, if the ERA5 covariate block were encoded in a form less accessible to the Transformer, or if a more expressive climate representation such as daily rather than 14-day aggregates recovered Transformer gains. The poor chlorophyll transfer finding would be partially explained, rather than invalidated, by retrieval-label noise inherited from the upstream hyperspectral model; the relative ordering of trait transferability (LAI $>$ LMA $>$ chlorophyll) is more robust than the absolute $R^2$ values. Confirmation that the Transformer encodes phenology implicitly will require representation-probing experiments that are not part of the present scope.

Several limitations bound the scope of our conclusions. First, the main experiments use labels from a single hyperspectral mission, EnMAP; the robustness of the findings to PRISMA-derived labels remains to be established. Second, the 30 m label spatial resolution is coarser than Sentinel-2's native 10--20 m bands, introducing a footprint mismatch that can attenuate trait--reflectance relationships. Third, the EnMAP retrieval validation error in the deployment domain is not yet reported in this Sentinel-2 draft; once available, the per-trait retrieval RMSE should be used to calibrate the attainable upper bound of Sentinel-2 supervised performance. Fourth, the study area is European and dominated by temperate-to-boreal forest contexts; tropical and dryland forest systems are not represented. Fifth, some ablations reuse hyperparameters selected at the 2,000 events per site-year baseline rather than refitting the grid at every ablation level, which may underestimate neural-model performance at higher sample caps. Sixth, formal residual-level statistical inference on paired ablation deltas remains incomplete in this technical draft.

These findings align with the broader goal of monitoring plant functional diversity from space \citep{jetz2016monitoring}, but they also show that trait upscaling from sparse labels requires explicit validation regimes. A defensible workflow should report in-domain accuracy, temporal transfer, spatial transfer, and label-source sensitivity as separate evidence streams. Future work will extend the label-source regime to a full EnMAP $\times$ PRISMA comparison, testing whether pooled hyperspectral supervision improves robustness or introduces sensor-specific retrieval bias. The accompanying gridded trait product and its multi-source evaluation are deferred to a companion data paper \citep{jiInPreparation}.

\section{Conclusion}

We present a trait-centered framework for learning forest functional traits from Sentinel-2 time series and sparse hyperspectral labels. The results support four methodological recommendations. First, construct supervised samples from finite trait-labelled events rather than optically valid pixels. Second, treat temporal representation and sample volume as explicit design axes. Third, evaluate climate reanalysis as a model-family-specific auxiliary stream. Fourth, report temporal and spatial transfer separately from grouped in-domain performance. Together, these recommendations define a reproducible basis for Sentinel-2 trait upscaling studies using sparse hyperspectral supervision.
