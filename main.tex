\title{Trait-centered Sentinel-2 time-series learning from sparse hyperspectral labels: a systematic ablation of design choices and generalization}

\author{Chaonan Ji\corref{cor1}}
\cortext[cor1]{Corresponding author}
\ead{chaonan.ji@uvsq.fr}
\address{EcoSpectra traits-model project}

\begin{abstract}
Forest functional traits link canopy structure and leaf biochemistry to ecosystem functioning, carbon exchange, and biodiversity, but spatially explicit trait observations remain sparse at landscape to continental scales. Sentinel-2 provides dense optical time series that could extend sparse hyperspectral trait labels, yet the design choices required for reliable trait-centered learning are rarely isolated. We evaluate a framework that anchors supervised samples on finite hyperspectral trait labels and links them to Sentinel-2 temporal windows under shared preprocessing, model-selection, and transfer-evaluation protocols. In a five-trait grouped benchmark, model skill was trait dependent: LAI reached the highest grouped performance ($R^2=0.645$, nRMSE $=8.5\%$ for MLP), whereas chlorophyll and lignin were weaker. In a separate B5/B6-filtered three-trait sample-size benchmark, mean nRMSE decreased from 9.49\% at cap2000 to 7.96\% at cap20000 for the complete XGBoost trajectory; Transformer improved from 9.44\% at cap2000 to 8.41\% at cap5000, but higher-cap neural rows differed in trait coverage and were not interpreted as a monotonic scaling curve. A sample-matched temporal-window experiment showed that 28 days gave the best mean performance among the evaluated windows ($R^2=0.497$, nRMSE $=10.39\%$). ERA5-Land climate context improved random forest and MLP performance ($\Delta R^2=0.027$ and 0.034, respectively) but had little average effect for the Transformer ($\Delta R^2=0.004$). Cross-year and cross-site evaluation reduced mean $R^2$ relative to grouped in-domain testing, especially for chlorophyll, demonstrating that temporal and spatial transfer must be reported as primary outcomes rather than diagnostics. These results show that Sentinel-2 trait upscaling from sparse hyperspectral labels is governed by interacting choices in representation, auxiliary information, and validation regime.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Plant functional traits summarize how vegetation acquires resources, grows, and responds to environmental stress. Traits such as leaf area index (LAI), leaf mass per area (LMA), foliar nitrogen, chlorophyll, and lignin are therefore central to interpreting ecosystem productivity, carbon cycling, and biodiversity patterns \citep{diaz2016global, jetz2016monitoring}. However, trait observations are sparse relative to the spatial and temporal heterogeneity of forests, and in situ sampling alone cannot support repeated regional monitoring.

Spaceborne optical remote sensing offers a route to broader trait monitoring. Imaging spectroscopy missions such as EnMAP and PRISMA provide spectrally rich observations that can be used to derive trait labels, while Sentinel-2 provides globally consistent, high-temporal-density multispectral observations \citep{drusch2012sentinel, guanter2015enmap, cogliati2021prisma}. A practical learning problem is therefore to use sparse hyperspectral trait labels as supervision for dense Sentinel-2 time series. This setting differs from standard supervised regression because labels are irregular in space and time, Sentinel-2 observations are affected by cloud-driven missingness, and model evaluation must distinguish in-domain accuracy from temporal and spatial transfer.

Several design choices remain under-examined in this setting. First, the supervised sample should be anchored on finite trait labels rather than all optically valid pixels, because a valid Sentinel-2 observation is not a trait observation. Second, the temporal representation of Sentinel-2 observations may control model performance as strongly as model architecture. Third, auxiliary information such as climate reanalysis may add mechanistic context, but its value may depend on the model family and its temporal inductive bias. Finally, grouped test performance does not establish robustness to new years or sites; transfer evaluation must be treated as a primary part of the method.

We address these gaps with a trait-centered learning framework and a systematic ablation study. The contribution is threefold. First, we define a reproducible sample construction that links sparse hyperspectral labels to Sentinel-2 temporal windows while preserving label masks and train-only preprocessing. Second, we compare random forest, sequence MLP, and Transformer models under shared sample, hyperparameter, and evaluation contracts. Third, we quantify the effects of sample size, temporal-window length, ERA5-Land climate context, and temporal/spatial transfer, thereby separating representation design, auxiliary information, and generalization from model-family effects.

\section{Data}

\subsection{Study sites and forest mask}

The analysis uses European forest sites with Sentinel-2 temporal coverage, hyperspectral trait-label support, ERA5-Land climate context, and land-cover information. Forest pixels are defined using Copernicus HRL 2021 tree-cover density with inclusion threshold $TCD \geq 30\%$. Sites with incomplete land-cover support are removed before model construction. WorldCover classes are retained for sensitivity analyses but do not define the primary forest mask.

\subsection{Trait labels and predictor streams}

Trait labels are sparse hyperspectral trait estimates derived from EnMAP scenes, with PRISMA providing an independent hyperspectral mission for label-source extension \citep{guanter2015enmap, cogliati2021prisma}. The supervised experiments reported here use the validated EnMAP trait-label contract. The five focal traits are LAI, LMA, nitrogen, chlorophyll, and lignin.

Sentinel-2 region zarrs provide dense optical predictor time series \citep{drusch2012sentinel}. Predictors include reflectance bands, spectral indices, day-of-year features, relative temporal offsets, and an observation mask or weight channel. ERA5-Land daily nearest-point data provide optional climate context matched to trait-centered samples \citep{munozsabater2021era5land}.

\subsection{Analysis cohorts}

The manuscript separates two analysis cohorts to avoid conflating data contracts. The five-trait grouped cohort is used to summarize baseline model-family behavior and the ERA5-Land paired ablation. The B5/B6-filtered WorldCover37 cohort is used for sample-size, temporal-window, and temporal/spatial transfer analyses; this cohort focuses on LAI, LMA, and chlorophyll because these traits have the most complete support in the filtered design.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Overview of the trait-centered learning framework}

The framework treats each finite trait-label event as the supervised anchor and links it to Sentinel-2 observations in a bounded temporal window (Fig.~\ref{fig:framework}). This design separates four scientific components: sparse hyperspectral supervision, dense Sentinel-2 temporal predictors, model-family comparison, and transfer evaluation. Ablations are then defined by changing one component while holding the sample construction, preprocessing, and evaluation contract fixed.

\centering

Sparse hyperspectral trait labels define supervised events; Sentinel-2 observations provide temporal optical predictors; model families are evaluated under grouped in-domain, temporal-transfer, and spatial-transfer regimes; and ablations test sample size, temporal representation, climate context, and transfer split design.}
\label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Trait-centered sample construction}

The training unit is a trait-labelled event defined by site, year, spatial location, acquisition time, label source, and finite target mask. Sentinel-2 observations are attached relative to the trait date. Any learned preprocessing, including imputers, feature scaling, and target transformations, is fit on the training partition only and then applied to validation and test partitions. Source provenance fields are retained so that single-source and multi-source label analyses can be evaluated with the same sample schema.

\subsection{Predictor representation}

The default representation is an eight-slot Sentinel-2 temporal sequence. Observations are ordered by temporal proximity to the hyperspectral label date. Missing slots are represented by zero-filled reflectance values together with an observation mask or weight, allowing the models to distinguish unobserved tokens from low-reflectance observations. Temporal-window ablations compare this default against shorter and longer windows under sample-matched conditions.

\subsection{Model architectures}

We compare three model families. Random forest provides a non-neural tree baseline \citep{breiman2001random}. The sequence MLP consumes the flattened temporal representation and the same mask/time features as the other models. The Transformer uses self-attention over temporal tokens following the sequence-modelling architecture introduced by \citet{vaswani2017attention}. XGBoost is included in sample-size and transfer ablations where it is the strongest available tree-family comparator, but the canonical interpretation focuses on the random forest, MLP, and Transformer families.

\subsection{Hyperparameter selection}

Hyperparameters are selected using validation nRMSE under the same split and sample contract as the corresponding experiment. Model-family settings are then fixed before test evaluation, and the test partition is used only for final reporting. For neural models, early stopping is based on validation loss. For tree models, the selected estimator configuration is refit on the training partition and evaluated on the held-out test partition with the same target transformation and preprocessing contract.

\subsection{Auxiliary information protocols}

ERA5-Land climate context is constructed as past-only climate information matched to each trait-labelled event. This prevents future climate information from entering the predictor stream. The climate ablation compares matched Sentinel-2-only and Sentinel-2-plus-ERA5 designs under paired trait, split, and model-family settings.

\subsection{Generalization protocol}

We distinguish three evaluation regimes. The grouped in-domain regime estimates performance when train and test samples are drawn from the same labelled era while grouping by pixel identity to avoid leakage. The temporal-transfer regime holds out target years and evaluates year-to-year transfer. The spatial-transfer regime holds out geographic site clusters and evaluates transfer across forest contexts. These regimes address different scientific questions and are reported separately.

\subsection{Statistical comparison protocol}

Ablation effects are reported as paired differences when treatment and baseline share the same trait, split, and model family. We use $\Delta R^2$ and $\Delta$NRMSE, with positive $\Delta R^2$ and negative $\Delta$NRMSE indicating improvement. For the ERA5-Land paired ablation, 95\% confidence intervals are estimated by bootstrap resampling across the five paired trait rows within each model family. Transfer heatmaps are reported as descriptive split-level summaries because they do not yet include a paired residual bootstrap.

\subsection{Computational cost reporting}

Computational cost is defined per model family as wall-clock time, accelerator type, number of fitted models, and approximate parameter count for neural models. These quantities are part of the reproducibility contract because sample-size and temporal-window ablations change both accuracy and training cost.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Baseline performance}

The five-trait grouped benchmark provides the reference point for the canonical model-family comparison. Performance varies strongly by trait and by model family, indicating that a single aggregate accuracy score would obscure meaningful structure in the results. MLP gives the best value for every trait in both metrics (asterisks in Fig.~\ref{fig:baseline_heatmap}), but the margin over Transformer is small for LAI, chlorophyll, and lignin. RF is consistently weaker in this benchmark, with the largest gap for lignin and LMA. Trait difficulty is also evident: LAI has the strongest grouped performance ($R^2=0.645$, nRMSE $=8.5\%$ for MLP), whereas chlorophyll and lignin remain below $R^2=0.50$ even for the best model.

\subsection{Design choices: data volume and temporal window}

The sample-size ablation tests whether prediction is limited by labelled-data volume in the B5/B6-filtered three-trait cohort. Mean test nRMSE decreases from 9.49\% at cap2000 to 7.96\% at cap20000 for the complete XGBoost trajectory. Transformer improves from 9.44\% at cap2000 to 8.41\% at cap5000. The apparent cap10000 Transformer value should not be interpreted as a deterioration in the same three-trait curve because that row contains only two traits, while the cap2000 and cap5000 rows contain all three. The defensible conclusion is therefore that sample scaling improves the complete XGBoost trajectory and improves Transformer through cap5000, but the available neural rows do not establish monotonic high-cap scaling.

The sample-matched temporal-window experiment controls for event availability by restricting all window lengths to the same sample cohort. Under this comparison, the 28-day window has the best mean performance among the evaluated windows (mean $R^2=0.497$, mean nRMSE $=10.39\%$), while the 0.5-day and 365-day windows are weaker (mean nRMSE $=10.66\%$ and $10.67\%$, respectively). Longer temporal context is therefore not automatically beneficial once the same labelled events are compared across windows.

\subsection{Auxiliary information streams}

ERA5-Land context improves the random forest and MLP more consistently than the Transformer. Across the five focal traits, the MLP gains average $\Delta R^2=0.034$ (95\% CI 0.027 to 0.040) and $\Delta$NRMSE $=-0.46$ percentage points (95\% CI -0.55 to -0.38), while random forest gains average $\Delta R^2=0.027$ (95\% CI 0.020 to 0.034) and $\Delta$NRMSE $=-0.36$ percentage points (95\% CI -0.48 to -0.26). The Transformer gain is much smaller and its confidence interval overlaps zero ($\Delta R^2=0.004$, 95\% CI -0.017 to 0.034; $\Delta$NRMSE $=-0.05$ percentage points, 95\% CI -0.42 to 0.18), suggesting that climate covariates are not uniformly useful across temporal architectures.

This model-family dependence suggests that ERA5-Land acts partly as an explicit phenological or stress-related shortcut. Tree models and MLPs can benefit from this added context, whereas the Transformer may already encode part of the same seasonal structure from the optical sequence.

\subsection{Generalization across years and sites}

Out-of-domain evaluation is substantially harder than grouped in-domain evaluation. In the B5/B6-filtered cross-year experiment, mean $R^2$ is 0.387 for MLP, 0.365 for Transformer, and 0.368 for XGBoost. Trait differences are large: LAI transfers best across years (mean $R^2=0.599$), LMA is intermediate (0.388), and chlorophyll is weak (0.134). This split-level evidence identifies transfer sensitivity but does not by itself separate input covariate shift from label distribution shift.

Spatial transfer is even more challenging in the site-cluster split. Mean $R^2$ falls to 0.279 for MLP, 0.245 for Transformer, and 0.233 for XGBoost. LAI remains the most transferable trait (mean $R^2=0.469$), LMA is moderate (0.284), and chlorophyll is near zero (0.004). These results show where transfer fails; attributing the failures to covariate shift, label shift, or changes in the reflectance--trait relationship requires additional distribution-overlap diagnostics.

\section{Discussion}

The main result is that Sentinel-2 trait learning from sparse hyperspectral labels is governed by representation and validation choices as much as by model architecture. This extends earlier trait-mapping and biophysical-retrieval work, which emphasized the potential of optical spectroscopy and machine-learning retrieval but also highlighted scaling, uncertainty, and operational robustness as persistent challenges \citep{homolova2013review, verrelst2015review}. Anchoring samples on finite trait labels addresses one part of that scaling problem by preventing optically valid but unlabelled pixels from entering the supervised target contract. The temporal-window ablation shows that more context is not inherently better; temporal context must be evaluated under matched sample availability because longer windows can add noisy or weakly relevant observations.

The ERA5-Land ablation shows that auxiliary information is model-family specific. The stronger gains for random forest and MLP suggest that explicit climate covariates can compensate for weaker temporal inductive bias. The weaker Transformer response is consistent with partial redundancy between climate covariates and phenological information already encoded in the Sentinel-2 sequence. This distinction matters for method design: adding climate context should be justified by paired ablations rather than adopted as a universal default.

The transfer experiments demonstrate that grouped in-domain accuracy is not sufficient evidence for robustness. Temporal transfer primarily tests year-to-year differences in phenology, climate, and label distribution, whereas spatial transfer also tests changes in forest composition, background conditions, and reflectance--trait relationships. The poor chlorophyll transfer performance highlights the need to separate trait distribution shift from model failure, especially for traits with lower signal-to-noise or narrower dynamic range in the training labels. The present transfer figures should therefore be read as robustness screening rather than a complete failure-mode attribution.

These findings align with the broader goal of monitoring plant functional diversity from space \citep{jetz2016monitoring}, but they also show that trait upscaling from sparse labels requires explicit validation regimes. A defensible workflow should report in-domain accuracy, temporal transfer, spatial transfer, and label-source sensitivity as separate evidence streams. Future multi-source experiments with EnMAP and PRISMA should test whether pooled hyperspectral supervision improves robustness or introduces sensor-specific retrieval bias.

\section{Conclusion}

We present a trait-centered framework for learning forest functional traits from Sentinel-2 time series and sparse hyperspectral labels. The results support four methodological recommendations. First, construct supervised samples from finite trait-labelled events rather than optically valid pixels. Second, treat temporal representation and sample volume as explicit design axes. Third, evaluate climate reanalysis as a model-family-specific auxiliary stream. Fourth, report temporal and spatial transfer separately from grouped in-domain performance. Together, these recommendations define a reproducible basis for Sentinel-2 trait upscaling studies using sparse hyperspectral supervision.
