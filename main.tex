\title{Trait-centered learning of forest functional traits from Sentinel-2 time series and sparse hyperspectral labels}

\author{Chaonan Ji\corref{cor1}}
\cortext[cor1]{Corresponding author}
\ead{chaonan.ji@uvsq.fr}
\address{EcoSpectra traits-model project}

\begin{abstract}
Predicting forest functional traits from Sentinel-2 time series using sparse hyperspectral labels requires explicit treatment of design choices that are often hidden inside preprocessing pipelines. We present a trait-centered training framework and a systematic ablation of three under-examined choices: temporal representation, auxiliary information, and out-of-domain generalization. The framework links EnMAP-derived trait estimates to Sentinel-2 observations through finite-label sample construction, a zero-plus-mask temporal representation, and train-only preprocessing contracts. We evaluate random forest, sequence multilayer perceptron, and Transformer models for leaf area index, leaf mass per area, nitrogen, chlorophyll, and lignin. The initial benchmark establishes strong trait dependence in predictive skill and motivates method-level ablations. Increasing the per-site-year sample cap improves accuracy for the three-trait B5/B6-filtered benchmark but shows diminishing returns between 5000 and 20000 samples per site-year. In a sample-matched temporal-window experiment, a 28-day window gives the best mean performance among the tested windows, indicating that longer temporal context is not automatically beneficial once sample availability is controlled. ERA5-Land climate context improves random forest and MLP performance but has weak and trait-specific effects for the Transformer, suggesting that auxiliary information is model-family specific. Cross-year and cross-site experiments show substantially lower transfer performance than grouped in-domain evaluation, especially for chlorophyll, indicating that temporal and spatial distribution shifts must be treated as first-class validation regimes. PRISMA is incorporated as the next label-source ablation axis, but this version reports only validated EnMAP-based training results.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Sentinel-2 provides dense optical time series that are well suited for monitoring seasonal vegetation dynamics, but supervised forest-trait modelling remains limited by the sparse availability of hyperspectral trait labels. EnMAP and PRISMA can provide spatially explicit trait estimates, yet these labels are irregular in space and time and are produced by their own retrieval pipelines. A Sentinel-2 trait model therefore has to solve more than a standard regression problem: it must connect sparse labelled events to temporal optical observations, handle missing observations, and remain interpretable under shifts in year, site, and label source.

This paper focuses on the methodological choices behind that task. We do not frame the current analysis as a delivered trait map. Instead, we ask which design choices are required before such a modelling system can be defended scientifically. In particular, we test whether predictive skill is controlled by the amount of labelled data, by the temporal representation of Sentinel-2 observations, by auxiliary information streams such as climate context or self-supervised temporal pretraining, and by out-of-domain evaluation across years and sites.

Our contribution is threefold. First, we define a trait-centered sample construction that starts from finite hyperspectral trait labels rather than from all valid pixels. This distinction is important because valid optical observations are not equivalent to supervised trait labels. Second, we compare a compact canonical model stack---random forest, sequence MLP, and Transformer---under shared sample, split, and preprocessing contracts. Third, we organize the evaluation into four thematic ablation blocks: baseline performance, data-volume and temporal-window design, auxiliary information streams, and temporal/spatial generalization. A fifth label-source ablation, EnMAP versus PRISMA versus pooled supervision, is specified as a required extension because it directly tests whether the model learns forest traits or sensor-specific retrieval bias.

\section{Data}

\subsection{Study sites and mask}

The analysis uses the EcoSpectra forest-site data path built around retained European sites with Sentinel-2, hyperspectral trait-label, climate, and land-cover support. The forest analysis mask is Copernicus HRL 2021 tree-cover density with inclusion defined as $TCD \geq 30$. Sites with land-cover coverage gaps are excluded from default model construction. WorldCover classes are retained only for diagnostic and sensitivity analyses, not as the canonical forest mask.

\subsection{Trait labels and predictors}

The supervised labels are sparse hyperspectral trait estimates. EnMAP is the validated label source for the results reported here. PRISMA L2D scenes have been downloaded and aligned to the common grid for a subset of sites, but PRISMA-derived trait maps are excluded from the current model results until the supervised trait-region contract is complete. The five gate traits are leaf area index (LAI), leaf mass per area (LMA), nitrogen, chlorophyll, and lignin.

Sentinel-2 region zarrs provide the dense optical predictor time series \citep{drusch2012sentinel}. The default sequence representation includes Sentinel-2 reflectance bands, spectral indices, day-of-year features, relative temporal offsets, and a validity or weight channel. ERA5-Land daily nearest-point data provide optional climate context for the 2022--2025 analysis period \citep{munozsabater2021era5land}.

\subsection{Trait-centered sample construction}

The training unit is a trait-labelled event defined by site, year, spatial location, acquisition time, and available trait mask. The construction starts from finite trait labels and then links each event to Sentinel-2 observations around the trait date. This rule prevents an important leakage in remote-sensing workflows: a valid optical pixel can still have no supervised trait label and must not be treated as a training target.

The sample contract preserves the label mask across all model families. Target scaling, imputers, normalizers, and any learned preprocessing are fit on the training partition only. Source provenance fields are retained when available so that EnMAP-only, PRISMA-only, pooled, and cross-source validation can be evaluated under the same framework in the next label-source ablation.

\subsection{Predictor representation}

The default representation is an eight-slot Sentinel-2 temporal window. Observations are ordered by temporal proximity to the trait acquisition. Missing slots are zero-filled and accompanied by an explicit observation mask or weight, allowing neural models to distinguish missing observations from low reflectance. This representation is deliberately simple: the data-volume and temporal-window ablations then test whether longer or denser temporal context is needed.

\subsection{Model architectures}

The canonical comparison uses three model families. Random forest is the non-neural tree baseline \citep{breiman2001random}. The sequence MLP consumes the flattened temporal representation while preserving the same mask features. The Transformer uses self-attention over the temporal tokens following the general sequence-modelling architecture of \citet{vaswani2017attention}. XGBoost, LightGBM, LSTM, and RNN variants are treated as supplementary extended comparisons rather than the central method claim.

\subsection{Auxiliary information protocols}

ERA5-Land climate context is constructed as past-only biweekly climate information matched to the trait-centered sample. This design tests whether external climate variables provide predictive information beyond the Sentinel-2 optical time series.

The ambient pretraining experiment uses self-supervised masked reconstruction of Sentinel-2 sequences before supervised trait fitting. The supervised Transformer arms compare a from-scratch checkpoint-compatible baseline with ambient-initialized encoders under frozen-encoder schedules. Here we interpret only the completed and consolidated auxiliary results; incomplete ambient runs are not used for numerical conclusions.

\subsection{Generalization protocol}

We distinguish three evaluation regimes. The grouped in-domain regime estimates performance when train and test samples are drawn from the same labelled era but grouped to avoid pixel-level leakage. The temporal-transfer regime holds out test years and evaluates year-to-year transfer. The spatial-transfer regime holds out geographic clusters and evaluates transfer across site groups. These regimes answer different questions and are not interchangeable: strong grouped performance does not imply temporal or spatial robustness.

\subsection{Statistical comparison protocol}

All model comparisons are reported as paired differences when a treatment and baseline share the same trait, split, and model family. We report $\Delta R^2$ and $\Delta$NRMSE, where positive $\Delta R^2$ and negative $\Delta$NRMSE indicate improvement. For the next revision, uncertainty intervals will be computed using bootstrap resampling over paired residual groups or paired split units, with multiple-comparison correction applied to the primary trait-level tests.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Baseline performance}

The five-trait benchmark establishes the reference point for later ablations. Predictive skill is strongly trait dependent: canopy and leaf-structure variables are more stable than pigment-related variables, and the ranking of model families depends on trait. This pattern motivates a systematic ablation rather than a single headline accuracy number.

\subsection{Design choices: data volume and temporal window}

The B5/B6-filtered sample-size experiment tests whether the current benchmark is limited primarily by labelled data volume. In the completed three-trait rows, mean test nRMSE decreases from approximately 9.49\% at cap2000 to 7.96\% for the cap20000 XGBoost arm, while the completed Transformer rows improve from 9.44\% at cap2000 to 8.41\% at cap5000. The neural cap50000 rows are excluded from the numerical conclusions because final summary files were unavailable at manuscript generation time.

The sample-matched temporal-window experiment controls for event availability by restricting all windows to the same cohort. Under this stricter comparison, the 28-day window has the best mean performance among the tested windows (mean $R^2=0.497$, mean nRMSE $=10.39\%$), while 0.5-day and 365-day windows are weaker (mean nRMSE $=10.66\%$ and $10.67\%$, respectively). Longer temporal context is therefore not automatically beneficial once the same labelled events are compared across windows.

\subsection{Auxiliary information streams}

ERA5-Land context improves the random forest and MLP more consistently than the Transformer. Across the five gate traits, MLP gains average $\Delta R^2=0.034$ and $\Delta$NRMSE $=-0.462$ percentage points, while random forest gains average $\Delta R^2=0.027$ and $\Delta$NRMSE $=-0.357$ percentage points. The Transformer gain is much smaller on average ($\Delta R^2=0.004$, $\Delta$NRMSE $=-0.051$ percentage points) and includes regressions for LAI, LMA, chlorophyll, and lignin.

This contrast suggests that auxiliary information interacts with architectural inductive bias. Tree models and MLPs may use climate variables as explicit shortcuts for phenological and stress-related variation. The Transformer already sees the temporal optical sequence and may encode some of the same seasonal structure, making ERA5 more redundant or even harmful for individual traits. Ambient masked pretraining targets a different auxiliary pathway: it changes the representation learned by the neural model rather than adding external covariates. The focused ambient arms are therefore retained as a neural-specific auxiliary experiment, but their final paired deltas are not used in this result text until the consolidated outputs are complete.

\subsection{Generalization across years and sites}

Out-of-domain evaluation is substantially harder than grouped in-domain evaluation. In the B5/B6-filtered cross-year experiment, mean $R^2$ over completed cap5000/cap20000 rows is 0.387 for MLP, 0.365 for Transformer, and 0.368 for XGBoost. Trait differences are large: LAI transfers best across years (mean $R^2=0.599$), LMA is intermediate (0.388), and chlorophyll is weak (0.134). This pattern is consistent with trait distribution drift and with lower signal-to-noise for pigment-related labels.

Spatial transfer is even more challenging in the current cluster split. Mean $R^2$ falls to 0.279 for MLP, 0.245 for Transformer, and 0.233 for XGBoost. Again, LAI is the most transferable trait (mean $R^2=0.469$), LMA is moderate (0.284), and chlorophyll is near zero (0.004). These results indicate that cross-site evaluation combines covariate shift in Sentinel-2 inputs, label shift in trait distributions, and possible concept shift in the reflectance--trait relationship across forest contexts.

\subsection{Label-source ablation}

Label source is a methodological design choice rather than a bookkeeping detail. A model trained on EnMAP-derived labels may learn both trait signal and retrieval-specific bias. PRISMA therefore enters the framework as a required label-source ablation with five intended arms: EnMAP-only, PRISMA-only, pooled EnMAP+PRISMA, EnMAP-to-PRISMA transfer, and PRISMA-to-EnMAP transfer. The minimum defensible version is a single-source versus pooled comparison. Because PRISMA trait-region outputs remain outside the validated supervised contract, this version reports the design and rationale but does not claim a PRISMA result.

\section{Discussion}

The main methodological result is that Sentinel-2 trait modelling is sensitive to how the labelled event is represented and evaluated. Baseline grouped-split performance is necessary but insufficient: the same model can look useful in-domain and weak under year or site transfer. The temporal-window experiment further shows that adding longer Sentinel-2 context is not automatically beneficial once sample availability is controlled, so temporal representation should be treated as a design axis rather than an assumed monotonic improvement.

Auxiliary information is also family specific. ERA5-Land context improves RF and MLP more than the Transformer, which suggests that external variables are most useful when the base architecture has weaker temporal inductive bias. This is a useful negative constraint: climate features should not be promoted as a universal default just because they improve some model families. Ambient pretraining tests a complementary hypothesis, namely whether a neural temporal encoder can be improved before supervised fitting, but final interpretation requires the consolidated ambient result table.

The generalization results point to three failure modes that should be separated in future work. Covariate shift occurs when held-out years or sites have different Sentinel-2 input distributions. Label shift occurs when the trait range differs between train and test partitions. Concept shift occurs when the same optical signal maps to different trait values across forest or climate contexts. Cross-year and cross-site diagnostics should therefore include trait-range overlap, input-distribution overlap, and residual structure rather than only average accuracy.

The major remaining evidence gap is label-source robustness. PRISMA has been prepared as a supplemental hyperspectral label source, but the supervised PRISMA trait contract must be completed before the EnMAP/PRISMA/pooled comparison can be interpreted. This ablation is essential for a methods paper because it tests whether pooled multi-source supervision improves robustness or introduces sensor-specific bias.

\section{Conclusion}

We present a trait-centered framework for learning forest functional traits from Sentinel-2 time series and sparse hyperspectral labels. The current evidence supports four methodological recommendations. First, start from finite trait-labelled events rather than valid optical pixels. Second, report temporal representation and sample-volume sensitivity rather than assuming a default window is harmless. Third, evaluate auxiliary information by model family because ERA5-Land gains are not uniform across architectures. Fourth, separate grouped in-domain performance from temporal and spatial transfer. These design choices form the basis for a defensible RSE methods paper and define the next required experiment: a label-source ablation across EnMAP, PRISMA, and pooled supervision.
